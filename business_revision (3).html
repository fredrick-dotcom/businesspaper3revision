Section A · Q1 · 8 Marks · Topic 1.4.1

Flexible Workforce

8 Marks 1.4.1 Approaches to Staffing Definition + 2× PEACH
"Assess two possible benefits of a flexible workforce for McVitie's."

📋 What is a Flexible Workforce?

A workforce where employees can vary their hours, contracts or roles to meet changing business needs.

Types of Flexible Working

  • Zero-hours contracts — no guaranteed hours; worker called in when needed
  • Part-time contracts — fewer than full-time hours; reduces fixed labour costs
  • Temporary contracts — fixed period; used for seasonal Christmas/Easter peaks
  • Multi-skilled workers — trained across multiple roles; can be redeployed
  • Annualised hours — total hours agreed per year but distributed flexibly

✅ Benefits

Cost Reduction

  • Only pay for hours needed during quiet periods
  • Avoids redundancy costs — temporary contracts simply end
  • Reduces overtime premium payments
  • Lower fixed labour cost base improves margins

Adaptability

  • Respond quickly to demand changes (new product launches)
  • Multi-skilled workers move between departments
  • Trial new products without permanent staffing commitment

⚠️ Drawbacks (H Points)

  • Quality risk — flexible staff may lack specialist training
  • Motivation — zero-hours workers have less loyalty (Herzberg: removes job security hygiene factor)
  • High turnover — increases recruitment and training costs
  • Reputational risk — zero-hours contracts attract negative press
  • Legal risk — employment law changes may restrict flexible contracts

McVitie's Context

  • Demand spikes: Christmas, Easter, Valentine's Day
  • Core products: Jaffa Cakes, Digestives, Hobnobs, Penguin
  • Growing demand for vegan/free-from = new production skills needed

🎓 Theorists

Taylor (Scientific Management)

Workers motivated by money — flexible pay-per-hour aligns with this view.

Herzberg (Hygiene Factors)

Job security is a hygiene factor. Zero-hours removes it, causing dissatisfaction even if pay is adequate.

Structure ReminderDefinition (1-2 sentences) → PEACH Paragraph 1 (Benefit 1) → PEACH Paragraph 2 (Benefit 2). No conclusion needed. Each paragraph MUST end with H.

🍑 PEACH Paragraph 1 — Cost Flexibility

P
Point
A flexible workforce allows McVitie's to reduce labour costs by aligning staffing levels with seasonal demand fluctuations.
E
Evidence
McVitie's (owned by Pladis) experiences significant demand spikes around Christmas and Easter — Jaffa Cakes and selection boxes. Temporary and zero-hours workers can be hired during peaks and released afterwards.
A
Analysis
This reduces fixed labour costs during quieter periods, protecting profit margins without incurring redundancy costs. Labour is one of the largest operating costs in food manufacturing — flexibility directly improves cost efficiency.
C
Context
For a large-scale manufacturer like McVitie's producing millions of units, even a small reduction in unit labour costs across seasonal peaks represents significant savings at scale.
H
Higher
However, over-reliance on flexible workers may reduce product consistency — temporary staff may lack specialist training of permanent employees. High staff turnover also increases recruitment costs, partially offsetting the savings.

🍑 PEACH Paragraph 2 — Adaptability

P
Point
A flexible workforce enables McVitie's to respond more quickly to changing consumer preferences and new product launches.
E
Evidence
Growing demand for vegan, gluten-free and reduced-sugar biscuits requires new production skills. A flexible workforce allows McVitie's to redeploy multi-skilled workers or bring in specialist temporary staff without restructuring permanently.
A
Analysis
This gives McVitie's a competitive advantage — the ability to trial new product lines rapidly without long-term cost commitment reduces the financial risk of innovation.
C
Context
In the confectionery industry, health and wellness trends are reshaping consumer demand rapidly. Businesses that adapt production quickly are better placed to capture new market segments before competitors.
H
Higher
However, allergen-free production lines require consistent, highly trained permanent staff. Flexibility has limits in specialist food manufacturing where quality and safety standards are tightly regulated.

Section A · Q2 · 10 Marks · Topics 2.5.1, 2.5.2

Taxation & McVitie's

10 Marks 2.5.1 Economic Influences Definition + PEACH + Conclusion
"Assess the effect on McVitie's of changes in taxation."

📋 Direct Taxes

  • Corporation Tax (25%) — tax on business profits; rose from 19% in 2023
  • Income Tax — reduces consumer disposable income → less confectionery spending
  • National Insurance — paid by employers on wages; a direct labour cost

📋 Indirect Taxes

  • VAT (20%) — processed confectionery is subject to VAT
  • Sugar Levy — introduced 2018 for soft drinks; lobbying pressure to extend to biscuits
  • Import Tariffs — tax on imported ingredients (cocoa, sugar, nuts)

⚡ Impact on McVitie's — Which Tax Matters Most?

Corporation Tax ArgumentDirectly reduces profit but doesn't affect demand. McVitie's can plan around it — Pladis has sophisticated transfer pricing strategies to mitigate impact.
Sugar Levy Argument ⭐Potentially MORE damaging — hits demand directly, not just profit. If biscuit demand falls due to higher prices, McVitie's faces a structural revenue problem harder to solve than a tax efficiency issue.

🎯 Strongest Conclusion

Sugar levy is the greater long-term threat because it attacks revenue; corporation tax attacks profit but leaves revenue intact.

Structure — 10 marksDefinition → PEACH 1 (Corporation Tax, full with H extended into counterpoint) → PEACH 2 (Sugar Levy) → Conclusion

🍑 PEACH 1 — Corporation Tax

P
Point
A rise in corporation tax directly reduces McVitie's post-tax profit, limiting funds available for reinvestment.
E
Evidence
UK corporation tax rose to 25% in 2023 — a significant increase affecting all profitable UK manufacturers. As a large-scale biscuit producer, even a small % rise represents a large cash outflow.
A
Analysis
Reduced post-tax profit means less retained earnings for R&D, new product development or capital investment. This could slow McVitie's ability to innovate and respond to health trends.
C
Context
For Pladis as a multinational, higher UK corporation tax may incentivise shifting production or profit-booking to lower-tax jurisdictions.
H
Higher
However, as a business with deep-rooted UK manufacturing, McVitie's cannot easily relocate. That said, Pladis has sophisticated tax planning strategies — transfer pricing may reduce the effective tax burden below the headline 25% rate.

🍑 PEACH 2 — Sugar Levy

P
Point
The potential extension of the sugar levy to biscuits would pose a significant threat to McVitie's revenue.
E
Evidence
The Soft Drinks Industry Levy was introduced in 2018 and faces lobbying for extension to biscuits. McVitie's core products — Digestives, Hobnobs, Jaffa Cakes — are high in sugar.
A
Analysis
McVitie's faces a dilemma: absorb the levy (squeezing margins) or pass it to consumers (risking demand loss). Biscuits have relatively elastic demand — many cheaper own-label substitutes exist.
C
Context
McVitie's already faces price pressure from supermarket own-label biscuits. A sugar levy accelerating the consumer shift to own-label would damage McVitie's market share.
H
Higher
However, if the levy applies equally to all biscuit manufacturers, the competitive landscape shifts uniformly — reducing the relative disadvantage to McVitie's specifically.

Conclusion

Taxation changes represent a significant threat to McVitie's profitability. Corporation tax rises reduce retained profit; the potential sugar levy threatens demand for its core product range. The greater long-term risk is the sugar levy — corporation tax affects profit but the sugar levy could fundamentally reshape consumer demand, hitting McVitie's at the revenue line rather than just the cost line.

🎯 Top TipThe sugar levy vs corporation tax debate is the core evaluation point — always argue which is more damaging and WHY.
🎯 Link to PEDMention price elasticity of demand — biscuits have elastic demand (many substitutes), so passing on a sugar levy through higher prices risks significant revenue loss.
🎯 Multinational AdvantagePladis can use transfer pricing to mitigate corporation tax — this is a strong H point showing sophisticated knowledge of MNC tax strategies.
Structure — 10 marksDefinition → Full PEACH 1 with extended H counterpoint → PEACH 2 → Conclusion taking a clear position on which tax matters most.

Pladis & Nigeria

12 Marks 4.4.1 Impact of MNCs Definition + 2× PEACH + Conclusion
"Assess the impact of the multinational company Pladis on the economy of Nigeria."

🌍 Key Context — Pladis & Nigeria

Pladis

  • Global snacking company owned by Yıldız Holding (Turkey)
  • Operates in 120+ countries
  • Brands: McVitie's, Godiva, Ulker
  • Sources cocoa from Nigeria and West Africa

Nigeria

  • MINT emerging economy
  • ~220 million population; rapidly growing middle class
  • One of world's largest cocoa producers
  • Developing economy — relies on commodity exports

✅ Positive Impacts

Employment

  • Direct jobs in cocoa farming, processing, logistics
  • Raises household incomes → multiplier effect
  • Local spending supports other businesses

Skills & Technology Transfer

  • Modern agricultural techniques shared
  • Local workforce becomes more productive long-term

GDP & Tax Revenue

  • Local sourcing adds to Nigerian economic output
  • Corporation tax revenues fund public services

⚠️ Negative Impacts

Profit Repatriation

  • Profits flow to Turkish shareholders, not reinvested locally
  • Long-term balance of payments damage
  • Nigeria captures limited long-term value

Low Wages / Exploitation

  • Buyer power drives down cocoa prices paid to farmers
  • Farmers may remain in poverty despite supplying a global brand

Tax Avoidance

  • Transfer pricing shifts profits to lower-tax jurisdictions
  • Nigeria loses corporation tax revenue
Structure — 12 marksDefinition of MNC → PEACH 1 (positive impact) → PEACH 2 (negative impact) → Conclusion

🍑 PEACH 1 — Positive Impact

P
Point
Pladis creates employment and stimulates economic activity in Nigeria through its supply chain operations.
E
Evidence
Nigeria is one of the world's largest cocoa producers and Pladis sources cocoa as a key ingredient. By sourcing locally, Pladis creates jobs across Nigeria's agricultural supply chain — from cocoa farming to processing and logistics.
A
Analysis
This raises household incomes for Nigerian workers, increasing consumer spending power and contributing to Nigeria's GDP through a multiplier effect.
C
Context
Nigeria is a MINT emerging economy with a rapidly growing population. MNC investment supports broader economic development goals — raising living standards in cocoa-producing regions.
H
Higher
However, wages in cocoa supply chains are often extremely low. If Pladis uses buyer power to keep cocoa prices low, the employment created may not meaningfully improve living standards, raising ethical concerns.

🍑 PEACH 2 — Negative Impact

P
Point
Pladis may repatriate profits back to its home country rather than reinvesting in Nigeria, reducing the long-term economic benefit to the host economy.
E
Evidence
Pladis is ultimately owned by Yıldız Holding, a Turkish conglomerate. Profits generated through Nigerian cocoa sourcing flow back to Turkish shareholders as dividends — not into the Nigerian economy.
A
Analysis
Nigeria's balance of payments suffers in the long run. While FDI inflows are positive initially, sustained profit repatriation year on year means the host country eventually loses more than it gains.
C
Context
Nigeria already faces profit outflows from foreign corporations in its oil sector. The confectionery supply chain adds another dimension — resource extraction (cocoa) with limited value retained domestically.
H
Higher
However, Pladis's operations do generate corporation tax revenues for Nigeria funding public services. Additionally, technology transfer may improve long-term agricultural productivity beyond the immediate relationship.

Conclusion

On balance, Pladis brings genuine short-term economic benefits — employment, income, GDP contribution and tax revenue. However, the long-term impact is ambiguous. Profit repatriation, low wages and limited domestic value creation suggest Nigeria captures only a fraction of the value generated by its cocoa resources. The net benefit depends on whether Pladis sources ethically, pays fair prices to farmers and invests meaningfully in local skills.

Section A · Q4 · 20 Marks ⭐ · Topic 3.3.2

Investment Appraisal

20 Marks ⭐ 3.3.2 Investment Appraisal Define + 4× PEACH + MOPS
"Using Extract D, evaluate both projects using ARR and NPV methods and recommend which one."

📈 Method 1: Payback Period

How long it takes for an investment to pay for itself from net returns. Businesses want the SHORTEST payback.

Payback = Sum Invested ÷ Net Cash per Time Period (equal flows)

Unequal Cash Flows (most common in exams)

  • Deduct each year's return from outstanding balance until zero
  • Remaining fraction: (Remaining ÷ Next year's cash flow) × 12 = months
  • Example: £50k invested, Y1=£13k, Y2=£28k, Y3=£27k → Payback = 2 years 4 months

📈 Method 2: Average Rate of Return (ARR) ⭐

Average annual profit as a % of the initial investment. Businesses want the HIGHEST ARR %.

ARR = (Average Annual Profit ÷ Initial Outlay) × 100
1
Total profit = Total returns MINUS initial investment
2
Average annual profit = Total profit ÷ Number of years
3
ARR = (Average annual profit ÷ Initial outlay) × 100
Worked ExampleInvestment £20k. Returns £10k/year for 5 years. Total profit = £30k. Annual avg = £6k. ARR = (£6k ÷ £20k) × 100 = 30%

📈 Method 3: Net Present Value (NPV) ⭐

Present-day value of all future cash flows minus the initial investment. Businesses want POSITIVE NPV — higher is better.

NPV = Sum of (Cash Flow × Discount Factor) for each year − Initial Investment
1
Multiply each year's cash flow by the discount factor given in the table
2
Sum all discounted cash flows
3
Subtract the initial investment
4
Positive NPV = worthwhile; Negative NPV = not worthwhile
Advantages of NPVMost sophisticated; accounts for time value of money; most accurate for large capital decisions
Disadvantages of NPVComplex; relies on accurate discount rate; if rates change, NPV figure becomes misleading
Structure — 20 marksDefine ARR + NPV → PEACH 1 (Project A ARR) → PEACH 2 (Project A NPV) → PEACH 3 (Project B ARR) → PEACH 4 (Project B NPV) → MOPS Conclusion

PEACH Template — Fill in from Extract D

P
Point
Project [A/B]'s ARR of [X]% indicates the average annual return — compare to bank interest rate and other project to judge value.
E
Evidence
Show calculation: Total profit ÷ years = annual avg → ARR = (annual avg ÷ outlay) × 100. Always write formula first, then substitute numbers.
A
Analysis
If ARR exceeds bank interest rate, the investment is financially worthwhile. A higher ARR suggests strong average annual profitability relative to cost.
C
Context
For McVitie's/Pladis, capital investment must generate returns that justify the opportunity cost — funds tied up cannot be used elsewhere.
H
Higher
However, ARR uses averages and ignores timing of cash flows. If returns are back-loaded, the average looks attractive but early years may create cash flow pressure — a significant weakness of this method.

🏁 MOPS Conclusion Template

M
Most Important
On the basis of both ARR and NPV, Project [A/B] is recommended as it offers the higher ARR of [X]% and the higher positive NPV of [£X].
O
Other Factors
However, Project [A/B] may offer advantages in payback speed / lower initial outlay — relevant if Pladis has cash flow constraints.
P
Proviso
This assumes the discount rate used in NPV remains appropriate. If interest rates rise significantly, the NPV figures would change.
S
Short vs Long
Short term: quicker payback reduces risk. Long term: higher NPV creates more shareholder value — Pladis should prioritise long-term value creation.

📖 Key Terms

TermDefinition
Investment AppraisalA scientific, quantitative approach to evaluating the financial consequences of an investment
Payback PeriodTime it takes for an investment to pay for itself from net returns — businesses want shortest payback
ARRAverage annual profit as a % of initial investment — formula: (Average Annual Profit ÷ Initial Outlay) × 100
NPVPresent-day value of all future cash flows minus initial investment — positive NPV = worthwhile
Discount FactorMultiplier applied to future cash flows — reflects that money today is worth more than money in the future
Time Value of Money£1 today is worth more than £1 in the future due to inflation, opportunity cost and risk
Net Cash FlowCash inflows minus cash outflows in a given period
Opportunity CostValue of the next best alternative foregone — capital tied up cannot be used elsewhere
Capital ExpenditureSpending on long-term assets — what investment appraisal is used to evaluate
🎯 Golden Rule 1Write the FORMULA FIRST before substituting numbers. Even if the final answer is wrong, method marks are awarded for correct formula and working.
🎯 Golden Rule 2Always INTERPRET after calculating: "This ARR of X% exceeds the current bank base rate, suggesting the investment is financially worthwhile."
🎯 Golden Rule 3Evaluate the METHOD itself: "However, ARR does not account for the timing of cash flows — a weakness when comparing projects with different return profiles."
🎯 Golden Rule 4Your MOPS conclusion MUST make a clear recommendation. Be decisive — state which project and why. Do not sit on the fence.
🎯 Qualitative FactorsEven after calculating, mention qualitative factors: strategic fit, employee impact, environmental credentials, market conditions. This shows depth of analysis beyond the numbers.

Section B · Q5 · 8 Marks · Topics 1.5.4, 2.1.3

Private Limited Company (Ltd)

8 Marks 1.5.4 Forms of Business Definition + 2× PEACH
"Assess two possible benefits to Lottie Shaw's of operating as a private limited company (Ltd)."

📋 Forms of Business — Know All Types

Sole Trader

  • One owner, full control
  • Unlimited liability — personal assets at risk
  • Difficult to raise large capital

Partnership

  • 2-20 partners, shared responsibility
  • Unlimited liability (unless LLP)

Ltd ⭐ THE FOCUS

  • Shares sold privately to known individuals
  • Limited liability for all shareholders
  • Share transfers require existing shareholder approval
  • Must file accounts at Companies House
  • Separate legal identity from owners

PLC

  • Shares traded publicly on stock exchange
  • Can raise very large capital but heavy regulation
  • Risk of hostile takeover

✅ Benefit 1 — Limited Liability

Shareholders can only lose the amount they invested — personal assets (home, savings) are protected if the business fails.

  • Expanding into a new bakery involves significant financial risk
  • Encourages owners to take calculated risks and invest
  • More willing to borrow when personal exposure is capped

✅ Benefit 2 — Access to Finance

  • Can sell shares to angel investors or private equity
  • Share transfers require existing shareholder approval — control maintained
  • Equity finance — no repayment required, no interest
  • Banks more willing to lend to a Ltd company
Structure — 8 marksDefinition of Ltd → PEACH 1 (limited liability) → PEACH 2 (access to finance). No conclusion. Strong H in each paragraph.

🍑 PEACH 1 — Limited Liability

P
Point
Operating as a Ltd gives Lottie Shaw's owners limited liability, protecting their personal assets if the business encounters financial difficulty.
E
Evidence
Unlike a sole trader where the owner could lose their home, Ltd shareholders can only lose the amount invested in shares. With Lottie Shaw's investing in a costly new bakery, the financial risk of this expansion is significant.
A
Analysis
Limited liability reduces personal financial risk, making owners more willing to take calculated risks — such as borrowing to fund the new bakery — knowing personal assets remain protected. This encourages entrepreneurial investment and growth.
C
Context
For a small Yorkshire artisan bakery expanding into a new bakery facility, limited liability makes such growth decisions less personally threatening for the founding owners.
H
Higher
However, Ltd status requires more complex accounting, auditing and reporting obligations. For a small business like Lottie Shaw's, these additional administrative costs and compliance requirements partially offset the benefit.

🍑 PEACH 2 — Access to Finance

P
Point
Ltd status allows Lottie Shaw's to raise finance by selling shares to new investors, without losing full control of the business.
E
Evidence
As a Ltd, Lottie Shaw's can bring in external shareholders — angel investors or private equity — to provide capital. Crucially, share transfers require approval of existing shareholders, so founders retain control over who joins.
A
Analysis
This provides equity finance — capital that does not need to be repaid and carries no interest cost — making it potentially less financially risky than a bank loan for the new bakery.
C
Context
Given the significant cost of a new bakery and the high interest rate environment, equity finance via share issue may be more sustainable for Lottie Shaw's than taking on substantial debt.
H
Higher
However, issuing new shares dilutes the existing owners' percentage stake and profit share. New shareholders expect returns — either through dividends or share value growth — creating a long-term obligation. The loss of some control may concern a family-run artisan business.

Section B · Q6 · 10 Marks · Topics 2.1.2, 2.3.2

Bank Loan Finance

10 Marks 2.1.2 External Finance Definition + PEACH + Conclusion
"Assess the value of a bank loan for Lottie Shaw's as a method of raising finance for its new bakery."

✅ FOR Bank Loan

  • Immediate lump sum — ideal for capital expenditure (bakery build)
  • Spreads cost over useful life of asset
  • Ownership and control retained — no new shareholders
  • Predictable monthly repayments aid cash flow planning
  • Interest payments are tax deductible — reduces actual cost
  • Builds credit history for future borrowing

⚠️ AGAINST Bank Loan

  • Interest cost makes total repayment exceed loan value
  • High UK interest rates post-2022 make borrowing expensive
  • Fixed repayments create cash flow pressure — must be paid in quiet periods
  • Collateral required — assets at risk if default
  • Worsens gearing ratio (increases financial risk)

⚡ Interest Rate Context — Critical!

  • Bank of England raised rates sharply from 2022 to control inflation
  • Rates reached 5.25% — highest in 15 years
  • A £500,000 loan at 7% over 15 years costs far more than the same loan at 2%
  • This context MUST be mentioned to score top marks

💡 Alternatives to Consider

  • Retained profit — no cost, but may be insufficient
  • Share issue — no repayment, but dilutes ownership
  • Government grants — free capital for small food businesses
  • William Jacksons parent funding — internal capital at lower cost
Structure — 10 marksDefinition of bank loan → PEACH 1 (FOR) with H extended into counterpoint → PEACH 2 (alternatives) → Conclusion

🍑 PEACH 1 — FOR the Bank Loan

P
Point
A bank loan provides Lottie Shaw's with an immediate lump sum specifically suited to large, one-off capital expenditure like a new bakery.
E
Evidence
Building or fitting out a new bakery requires significant upfront investment in property and industrial equipment — costs that cannot easily be covered from retained profit for a small artisan business. A 10-15 year loan spreads repayment over the useful life of the asset.
A
Analysis
This allows Lottie Shaw's to invest now and generate returns from the new bakery while repaying — using future revenue to fund the investment. Predictable monthly repayments make cash flow planning straightforward.
C
Context
Lottie Shaw's is a small Yorkshire artisan bakery with limited retained profits. A bank loan allows access to capital at a scale impossible from internal sources alone — enabling growth otherwise unachievable.
H
Higher
However, in the current high interest rate environment (rates reached 5.25%), the total cost of borrowing is substantially higher than in the 2010s. Fixed repayments create cash flow pressure during seasonal quiet periods — if revenue falls short, loan repayments must still be met, risking default and loss of collateral.

Conclusion

A bank loan has genuine value as it provides the scale of capital required that internal sources cannot match. However, in the current high-interest environment, the cost is significant and fixed repayments create cash flow risk for a seasonal business. The value depends on Lottie Shaw's existing cash flow strength, the interest rate negotiated, and whether alternative sources are available. A blended finance approach (retained profit + grant + smaller loan) would be ideal.

UK-EU Trade Deal

12 Marks 4.1.5 Trading Blocs Definition + 2× PEACH + Conclusion
"Assess the effect on Lottie Shaw's of the UK's trade deal with the EU trading bloc."

🌍 Key Context

EU Single Market

  • 27 member states, ~450 million consumers
  • Free movement of goods, services, capital and people
  • UK left in 2020 (Brexit)
  • UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA) — zero tariffs IF rules of origin met

Lottie Shaw's Context

  • Small Yorkshire artisan bakery — primarily domestic focus
  • Premium positioning — potential for European premium food market
  • Parent: William Jacksons — may provide export support
  • Limited management capacity for compliance burden

✅ Positive Effects

Export Opportunity

  • Reduced tariffs on UK food exports opens European market
  • EU consumers in France, Germany pay premium for British artisan food
  • Yorkshire heritage is a marketable story in European premium markets
  • E-commerce allows SMEs to reach EU consumers directly

Import Cost Reduction

  • Cheaper EU ingredients (butter, nuts, flour) lowers input costs
  • Improves gross profit margin

⚠️ Negative Effects

Increased Competition

  • Belgian chocolate, French patisserie, German biscuits become more price-competitive in UK
  • Intensifies competition in Lottie Shaw's core domestic market

Regulatory Burden

  • Dual regulatory compliance (UK + EU standards) costly and complex
  • Rules of origin — ingredients from outside UK/EU may not qualify
  • Currency risk if selling in euros
Structure — 12 marksDefinition of trading bloc → PEACH 1 (positive effect) → PEACH 2 (negative effect) → Conclusion

🍑 PEACH 1 — Positive Effect

P
Point
A UK-EU trade deal that reduces tariffs on food exports could open significant new markets for Lottie Shaw's products across Europe.
E
Evidence
A favourable trade deal restoring reduced-tariff access allows Lottie Shaw's to export its premium biscuits to European consumers — particularly in France and Germany where British artisan food commands premium pricing.
A
Analysis
Access to the EU's 450 million consumers represents a Market Development opportunity (Ansoff) — existing products, new markets. Even a small share of European premium biscuit demand could significantly boost revenue.
C
Context
Lottie Shaw's Yorkshire heritage positioning — handmade, quality ingredients — suits European premium food markets where British artisan products command above-average prices.
H
Higher
However, exporting to the EU requires compliance with EU food labelling, allergen and packaging regulations. The administrative burden of navigating dual regulatory requirements could be disproportionately costly for a small business, limiting the practical benefit.

🍑 PEACH 2 — Negative Effect

P
Point
A UK-EU trade deal also increases competition in the UK domestic market by making it easier for EU producers to export to the UK.
E
Evidence
The EU has major confectionery and bakery producers — Belgian chocolate, French patisserie, German biscuits — that benefit from reduced UK tariffs. With trade barriers lowered, these become more price-competitive in UK supermarkets.
A
Analysis
This intensifies competition for Lottie Shaw's in its core UK market. Lottie Shaw's faces increased pressure on both volume and pricing — particularly in the premium gifting segment.
C
Context
Lottie Shaw's is small and predominantly domestically focused. Unlike a multinational, it lacks resources to compete on a pan-European scale. The competitive threat from EU imports is more immediately felt than any export opportunity.
H
Higher
However, Lottie Shaw's Yorkshire heritage story and local provenance are difficult for EU competitors to replicate. Consumers who value local British provenance are unlikely to switch simply because import tariffs fall — the threat may be more limited in practice than in theory.

Conclusion

The net effect is likely modestly negative in the short term. As a small, domestically-focused business, Lottie Shaw's is more exposed to EU import competition than positioned to capitalise on European export opportunities. However, long-term, its premium Yorkshire heritage brand has genuine European potential — if it can navigate the regulatory requirements.

Section B · Q8 · 20 Marks ⭐ · Topics 3.2, 1.3.4

Growth Strategy

20 Marks ⭐ 3.2 Growth / 1.3.4 Distribution Define Both + 4× PEACH + MOPS
"William Jacksons has set the objective of expanding Lottie Shaw's by increasing the distribution of its products built on a multi-channel approach OR by producing own-label biscuits."

📦 Option 1: Multi-Channel Distribution

Selling through multiple routes to market simultaneously.

Available Channels

  • Own website / e-commerce (DTC)
  • Online marketplaces (Not On The High Street, Etsy)
  • Independent retailers, farm shops, delis
  • Regional supermarkets (Booths, Waitrose)
  • Subscription gifting boxes
  • Wholesale to hospitality

Ansoff: Market Development

Existing products, new markets. Medium risk.

🏪 Option 2: Own-Label Production

Manufacturing products sold under a retailer's brand name.

How It Works

  • Retailer specifies product; Lottie Shaw's manufactures it
  • Sold under retailer's label (e.g. Waitrose, M&S)
  • Fixed price per unit — typically low margin
  • High guaranteed volumes

Ansoff: Product + Market Development

New product variant, potentially new customer base. Medium-high risk.

✅ Multi-Channel Advantages

  • Reaches far more customers nationally
  • Builds brand equity — Lottie Shaw's name visible everywhere
  • Diversifies revenue — less dependency on single channel
  • E-commerce has low marginal cost once set up
  • Retains full pricing power
  • Supports long-term brand building

⚠️ Multi-Channel Disadvantages

  • Operationally complex — different logistics per channel
  • Upfront investment in platform, packaging, fulfilment
  • Risk of overtrading alongside new bakery build
  • Channel conflict risk

✅ Own-Label Advantages

  • Guaranteed high-volume orders — predictable revenue
  • Immediate bakery capacity utilisation
  • Reduced marketing cost — retailer handles promotion
  • Cash flow security supports loan repayments
  • William Jacksons parent relationships facilitate contracts

⚠️ Own-Label Disadvantages

  • Thin margins — retailer captures most value
  • Brand invisibility — Lottie Shaw's name disappears
  • Brand dilution risk if discovered by loyal customers
  • Single customer dependency risk
  • Contradicts artisan heritage USP
Structure — 20 marksDefine multi-channel + own-label → PEACH 1 (MC: FOR) → PEACH 2 (MC: AGAINST) → PEACH 3 (OL: FOR) → PEACH 4 (OL: AGAINST) → MOPS Conclusion

🍑 PEACH 1 — Multi-Channel: FOR

P
Point
A multi-channel strategy allows Lottie Shaw's to reach a significantly larger customer base, driving revenue growth without changing its core brand identity.
E
Evidence
Combining Yorkshire shops and website with online marketplaces like Not On The High Street or subscription gifting allows Lottie Shaw's to reach customers nationally who would never visit Yorkshire but value premium artisan products.
A
Analysis
This is Market Development (Ansoff) — reaching new customers with existing products. Each additional channel adds incremental revenue with low marginal cost once the new bakery is operational. Lottie Shaw's retains full pricing power across all channels.
C
Context
E-commerce and premium gifting platforms have created national demand for artisan food. Lottie Shaw's Yorkshire heritage story suits online storytelling through social media and DTC channels — which cost relatively little to establish.
H
Higher
However, managing multiple channels simultaneously is operationally complex — different logistics, customer expectations and margin structures per channel. A small business may lack the management capacity to run multi-channel effectively without quality standards slipping.

🍑 PEACH 3 — Own-Label: FOR

P
Point
Own-label production provides Lottie Shaw's with guaranteed high-volume orders and stable, predictable revenue — directly supporting the financial case for the new bakery.
E
Evidence
A supply contract with Waitrose or M&S could provide guaranteed order volume that fills the new bakery's capacity immediately upon opening — eliminating demand uncertainty of a multi-channel approach.
A
Analysis
Predictable revenue makes it significantly easier to service the bank loan financing the new bakery. Higher production volumes reduce unit costs through economies of scale — improving margins even at lower own-label prices.
C
Context
William Jacksons as parent company likely has existing retailer relationships that could facilitate an own-label agreement — reducing commercial risk considerably versus a start-up seeking its first retail contract.
H
Higher
However, own-label contracts demand very thin margins — the retailer captures most consumer-facing value using buyer power to drive down supplier prices. Lottie Shaw's would be producing artisan-quality biscuits for a fraction of what it earns under its own brand — a poor use of premium capability long-term.

🏁 MOPS Conclusion

M
Main Judgement
Multi-channel distribution is the stronger long-term strategy. It builds brand equity, diversifies revenue and aligns with growing consumer demand for authentic, direct brand relationships. The new bakery's capacity is best deployed amplifying the Lottie Shaw's brand — not invisibly filling a supermarket's own-label shelf.
O
Other Factors
Own-label offers compelling short-term advantages — guaranteed volume, predictable revenue and immediate bakery utilisation. These are not insignificant for a small business managing a major capital investment.
P
Proviso
If Lottie Shaw's primary short-term objective is financial stability and loan repayment security, own-label may be a necessary transitional strategy — provided it is time-limited and kept separate from the branded business. The recommendation changes if cash flow is constrained.
S
Short vs Long Term
Short term: own-label reduces financial risk. Long term: multi-channel builds a sustainable, premium brand with pricing power and customer loyalty. William Jacksons should prioritise the strategy that builds Lottie Shaw's long-term brand value.

PEACH & MOPS Framework

Essential Exam Technique

🍑 PEACH — Every Analytical Paragraph

P — Point

Make your argument clearly in one sentence.

E — Evidence

Data, named business example, or theory. Name real companies!

A — Analysis

Explain the cause-and-effect chain. Why does the evidence support the point?

C — Context

Link specifically to the business/industry in the question.

H — Higher Level Development (THE KEY TO TOP MARKS)

Extend into a counterpoint, qualification, or second-order effect. This is what separates a B from an A.

🏁 MOPS — 20-Mark Conclusions Only

M
Most Important
Your overall judgement — be decisive. State which option/factor is more significant and why.
O
Other Factors
Acknowledge the counter-argument. What advantages does the other option have?
P
Proviso
Under what circumstances might your answer change? What assumptions are you making?
S
Short vs Long Term
Does the answer differ in the short vs long term? Usually yes — use this to add depth.

📊 Mark Scheme — Question Structures

4m
Explain: Definition → 2 pieces of evidence → Analytical point
8m
Assess (Paper 3): Definition → 2 full PEACH paragraphs (no conclusion needed)
10m
Assess: Definition → PEACH with H extended into counterpoint → Conclusion
12m
Assess: Definition → 2 PEACH paragraphs (one for, one against) → Conclusion
20m
Evaluate: Define both options → 2 PEACH per option (4 total) → MOPS conclusion

Porter's Five Forces — Confectionery

1. Threat of New Entrants — LOW-MEDIUM

  • High capital needed for manufacturing at scale
  • Strong brand loyalty to Cadbury, Mars, Nestlé
  • BUT: Low barriers for artisan/online entrants
  • E-commerce reduces distribution barriers for small brands

2. Threat of Substitutes — HIGH

  • Snack bars, fruit, crisps all compete for treat spend
  • Health trend drives consumers away from confectionery
  • Premium gifting alternatives (flowers, candles)

3. Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MEDIUM-HIGH

  • Cocoa from few regions — Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire dominate (60%+ of world supply)
  • Climate change threatens supply — prices volatile
  • Large firms negotiate better terms than SMEs

4. Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH

  • Supermarkets (Tesco, Sainsbury's) demand low prices
  • Consumers price-sensitive in cost-of-living squeeze
  • BUT: Premium buyers (Hotel Chocolat customers) less price-sensitive

5. Competitive Rivalry — VERY HIGH

  • Mondelez, Mars, Nestlé, Ferrero fight for supermarket shelf space
  • Heavy advertising spend needed to maintain market share
  • Price wars between own-label and branded
  • Saturated UK market — growth must come from innovation or international expansion
